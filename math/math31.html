Calculus Exercises

1. Integration

1.1. Sum Notation

Sum notation saves mathematicians from writing a lot of dots. Instead of writing \(a_1+a_2+\cdots+a_n\), sum notation can be put to good use to write \[\sum_{i=1}^na_i\] instead. The summation of the \(a_i\) is performed as the index \(i\) ranges from \(1\) to \(n\).

  1. Exercise 1: Write \(a_1+a_2+\cdots+a_5\) in sum notation.
    1. This is simply \(\sum_{i=1}^5 a_i\).
  2. Exercise 2: Suppose we have a sequence \(a_1, a_2,\ldots\). Write the sum of the first \(5\) members with even index as a sum and in sum notation.
    1. Writing it out would be \(a_2+a_4+a_6+a_8+a_{10}\).
    2. Since the index goes from beginning to end in steps of \(1\), the selection has to be done at the \(a_i\), where we now only use the
    3. \(a_{2i}\), so
    4. \[\sum_{i=1}^{5}a_{2i}\] is the sum notation expression.
  3. Exercise 3: Write the sum of the first \(50\) elements of \((a_1, a_2,\ldots)\) with odd indices in sum notation.
    1. Again, the indices have to be adjusted at the \(a_i\) where now only indices of the form
    2. \(2i+1\) are selected to give
    3. \[\sum_{i=1}^{50}a_{2i+1}.\]
  4. Exercise 4: Write out \(\sum_{k=1}^5\left(\frac{k}{5}\right)^2\). Is there any room for simplification?
    1. \[\sum_{k=1}^5\left(\frac{k}{5}\right)^2=\frac{1^2}{5^2}+\frac{2^2}{5^2}+\frac{3^2}{5^2}+\frac{4^2}{5^2}+\frac{5^2}{5^2}.\]
    2. Since \(\frac{1}{5^2}\) occurs unchanged in every term of the sum, it can be factored out to simplify this to
    3. \[\sum_{k=1}^5\left(\frac{k}{5}\right)^2=\frac{1}{5^2}\sum_{k=1}^5k^2.\]
  5. Factoring of Sums: In general, terms not depending on the index can be factored out of the sum. Try this with \(\sum_{k=0}{n}kn^2\).
    1. \(\sum_{k=0}{n}kn^2=n^2\sum_{k=0}{n}k\) since \(n\) doesn't depend on the index.
  6. Splitting Sums: Convince yourself that \[\sum_{i=0}^n(a_i+b_i)=\sum_{i=0}^na_i+\sum_{i=0}^nb_i\] by writing out the case \(n=4\).
  7. Index Splits: Convince yourself that \[\sum_{i=0}^na_i+\sum_{i=n+1}^ma_i=\sum_{i=0}^ma_i\] for \(m\ge n+1\) by writing this out for \(n=3\), \(m=5\).

1.2. Types of Integrals

In calculus of one variable there are three types of integrals to deal with:

Indefinite Integrals
These are essentially antiderivatives, written \(\int f(x)dx\) with an integral sign without boundaries. When asked to calculate \(\int f(x)dx\) you are supposed to find a function \(F(x)\) such that \(F'(x)=f(x)\) and then provide \(F(x)+c\) with \(c\) an unspecified constant as the result of your efforts, ie \[\int f(x)dx=F(x)+c\] for an unspecified constant \(c\) and a function \(F(x)\) satisfying \(F'(x)=f(x)\).
Definite Integrals
These are about calculating areas between the graph of a function and the \(x\)-axis over an interval \([a,b]\), written \[\int_a^bf(x)dx.\] Note that areas where \(f(x)<0\) will be negative. While they can be calculated using the limit of a sum of rectangles approaching the area, the easier way is to get an antiderivative \(F(x)\) of \(f(x)\). Then \[\int_a^bf(x)dx=F(b)-F(a).\]
Improper Integrals
These are limits of definite integrals where a boundary may be infinite or located at a point where the function is not defined. For example \[\int_0^1\frac{1}{x^2}dx\] is improper because the function is not defined at the lower boundary. Similarly, \[\int_1^\infty\frac{1}{x^2}dx\] because here the upper bound is at infinity. Even worse, \[\int_0^\infty\frac{1}{x^2}dx,\] which has problems at both ends.

1.3. Indefinite Integrals

An indefinite integral \(\int f(x) dx\) is an antiderivative of \(f(x)\), that is, a function \(F(x)\) such that \(F'(x)=f(x)\). Since these antiderivatives can differ by a constant \(C\), which becomes \(0\) when taking the derivative, the integral is written as \[\int f(x)dx=F(x)+C.\] For example, if \(f(x)=x\), \[\int f(x)dx=\int xdx=\frac{1}{2}x^2+C.\]

1.3.1. Exercises 1: Some Simple Integrals

Click on a questions for hints on how to solve it.

  1. Calculate \(\int 2dx\).
    1. The function here is the constant function \(f(x)=2\), so \(F(x)\)
    2. is \(F(x)=2x\) and the integral becomes
    3. \[\int 2dx=2x+C.\]
  2. Calculate \(\int dx\).
    1. Here the function is \(f(x)=1\), since, in a quirk of notation, \(\int 1dx\) is written as merely \(\int dx\).
    2. Thus \(F(x)=x\) and the result is
    3. \[\int dx=x+C.\]
  3. Calculate \(\int 1/x^2dx\).
    1. Remember that \(1/x^n=x^{-n}\) and that
    2. the rule \[\frac{dx^n}{dx}=nx^{n-1}\] als holds for negative \(n\).
    3. Therefore \[\frac{dx^{-1}}{dx}=(-1)x^{-2},\]
    4. so that \(-x^{-1}\) is an antiderivative of \(x^{-2}\).
    5. Thus, \[\int\frac{1}{x^2}dx=\int x^{-2}dx=-x^{-1}+C=-\frac{1}{x}+C.\]
  4. Calculate \(\int 1/x^3dx\).
    1. With \(f(x)=x^{-3}\), an antiderivative is
    2. \(F(x)=-1/2x^{-2}\), so
    3. \[\int \frac{1}{x^3}dx=\int x^{-3}dx=-\frac{1}{2}x^{-2}+C=-\frac{1}{2x^{-2}}+C.\]
  5. Calculate \(\int\sqrt{x}dx\).
    1. Remember that \(\sqrt{x}=x^{1/2}\) and that
    2. the rule \[\frac{dx^n}{dx}=nx^{n-1}\] als holds for fractional \(n\).
    3. Therefore, \[\frac{1}{3/2}x^{\frac{3}{2}}=\frac{2}{3}\sqrt{x^3}=\frac{2}{3}\sqrt{x}^3\] is an antiderivative of \(\sqrt{x}\) and
    4. \[\int\sqrt{x}dx=\frac{2}{3}\sqrt{x}^3+C.\]

1.3.2. Exercises 2: Integrals of Sums

Sums of functions can be integrated term-wise, which means \[\int f(x)+g(x) dx=\int f(x)dx+\int g(x)dx.\] For example \[\int x+x^2 dx = \int xdx+\int x^2dx=\frac{1}{2}x+\frac{1}{3}x^3+C.\] Note that, in the absence of parentheses, \(dx\) indicates where the integration ends, so \[int x dx+ x^2=\frac{1}{2}x^2+x^2+C=\frac{3}{2}x^2\] because here the integration ranges only over \(f(x)=x\).

  1. Evaluate \(\int 6x^5+18x^2+7dx\).
  2. Evaluate \(\int 6x^5dx+18x^2+7\).
  3. Evaluate \(\int 6x^5+18x^2dx+7\).
  4. Evaluate \(\int (x^2+6x-7)dx\).
  5. Evaluate \(\int 40x^3+12x^2-9x+14dx\).
  6. Evaluate \(\int 40x^3+12x^2-9xdx+14\).
  7. Evaluate \(\int 40x^3+12x^2dx-9x+14\).
  8. Evaluate \(\int 40x^3dx+12x^2-9x+14\).
  9. Determine \(f(x)\) for \(f'(x)=6x^8-20x^4+x^2+9\).

1.3.3. Exercises 3: Integration Variables

The variable \(x\) in \(dx\) determines which variable to integrate in an expression. For example \[\int t+t^2dx=x(t+t^2\)+C\] because the expression \(t+t^2\) is constant with respect to the integration variable \(x\). However \[\int t+t^2dt=\frac{1}{2}t^2+\frac{1}{3}t^3+C.\]

  1. Evaluate \(\int 12t^7-t^2-t+3dt\).
  2. Evaluate \(\int 10w^4+9w^3+7wdw\).
  3. Evaluate \(\int z^6+4z^4-z^2dz\).
  4. Determine \(f(t)\) for \(f'(t)=t^4-t^2+t-1\).
  5. Evaluate \(\int x^3+3tx^2+t^2x+t^3dx\).
  6. Evaluate \(\int x^3+3tx^2+t^2x+t^3dt\).

1.3.4. Exercises 3: Changing Variables

Changing integration variables often simplifies integration considerably. Consider something like \(\int (3x+5)^{10}dx\). This can be solved, at considerable effort, by multiplying out the expression \((3x+5)^{10}\). On the other hand, this integral has the form \(\int u^10dx\), so things would be much easier if just \(u^10\) could be integrated as \[\int (3x+5)^{10}dx=\int u^{10} dx\] with \(u=3x+5\). Unfortunately, the integration variable is still \(x\) and not \(u\), so it needs to be changed if possible. With \[\frac{du}{dx}=3,\] the expression \(u^10=(3x+5)^{10}\) can be rewritten as \[u^10=\frac{1}{3}u\frac{du}{dx}u^{10}\] and the integral can be rewritten as \[\int \frac{1}{3}u^{10}\frac{du}{dx}dx=\int\frac{1}{3}u^{10}du=\frac{1}{11}u^{11}+C=\frac{1}{11}(3x+5)^{11}+C.\]

  1. Use a change of variables to integrate \(\int (x+1)^2dx\).
  2. Integrate \(\int x^2+2x+1dx\) and compare to the previous result.
  3. Use a change of variables to integrate \(\int (3x+1)^5dx\).
  4. Use a change of variables to integrate \(\int (5x+1)^3dx\).
  5. Use a change of variables to integrate \(\int (1/3x+1)^{10}dx\).
  6. Use a change of variables to integrate \(\int (7x+1)^9dx\).
  7. Use a change of variables to integrate \(\int \frac{1}{(7x+3)^2}dx\).
  8. Use a change of variables to integrate \(\int (\sqrt{7x+3}dx\).
  9. Is it possible to integrate \(\int (x^2+1)^2dx\) by a change of variables? Integrate with integrant multiplied out and compare results.
  10. Is it possible to integrate \(\int x(x^2+1)^2dx\) by a change of variables? Integrate with integrant multiplied out and compare results.

1.3.5. Exercises 4: Integrals of Products

1.4. Definite Integrals

Definite integrals are defined as the limit of sums \[\int_a^bf(x)dx=\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\sum_{i=0}^nf(\xi_i)(x_{i+1}-x_i),\] where \(\xi_i\in[x_i,x_{i+1}]\). The idea is to split up the interval \([a,b]\) into smaller intervals \([x_i,x_{i+1}]\) and then to approximate the area between \(f(x)\) and the \(x\)-axis by the rectangles \(f(\xi_i)(x_{i+1}-x_i)\). Depending on the choice of \(\xi_i\in[x_i,x_{i+1}]\), the rectangle may be completely between the graph of \(f(x)\) and the \(x\)-axis or it may contain parts of it. In the limit this shouldn't make a difference.

Often, the intervals \[[x_i,x_{i+1}]\] are chosen to have equal length \(\Delta x=x_{i+1}-x_i\) in which case the sum above is written \[\int_a^bf(x)dx=\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\sum_{i=0}^nf(\xi_i)\Delta x.\] With increasing \(n\), the \(x_i\) are getting tighter and tighter and \(\Delta x\) becomes infinitesimal, explaining the \(dx\) in the integral.

  1. Area under the Parabola: To illustrate this, calculate the area under the parabola \(f(x)=x^2\) on the interval \([0,1]\) by dividing \([0,1]\) into subintervals \([\frac{i}{n},\frac{i+1}{n}]\) and choosing \(\xi_i\) to be the left endpoint of its corresponding interval. Write down the sum for this.
    1. In the sum \[\sum_{i=0}^nf(\xi_i)(x_{i+1}-x_i)\]replace \(x_i=\frac{i}{n}\) and \(\xi_i=\frac{i}{n}\). Then \[x_{i+1}-x_i=\frac{i+1}{n}-\frac{i}{n}=\frac{1}{n}\] and the sum becomes
    2. \[\sum_{i=0}^nf\left(\frac{i}{n}\right)\frac{1}{n}\] and, since \(f(x)=x^2\),
    3. \[\sum_{i=0}^n\frac{i^2}{n^2}\frac{1}{n},\] which simplifies to
    4. \[\frac{1}{n^3}\sum_{i=0}^ni^2,\] requiring the calculation of the sum of squares. A quick search on Google reveals that
    5. \[\sum_{i=0}^ni^2=\frac{1}{3}n^3+\frac{1}{2}n^2+\frac{1}{6}n\] and our sum is
    6. \[\frac{1}{n^3}\sum_{i=0}^ni^2=\frac{1}{3}+\frac{1}{2n}+\frac{1}{6n^2}.\] In the limit as \(n\rightarrow\infty\) this becomes
    7. \[\int_0^1x^2dx=\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\left(\frac{1}{3}+\frac{1}{2n}+\frac{1}{6n^2}\right)=\frac{1}{3}.\]
