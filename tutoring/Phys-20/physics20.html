1. Mathematical Vector Notation in 2D

In two dimensions, vectors are arrows in the plane. The most common ways to describe vectors are polar representation and cartesian representation.

1.1. Polar Vectors

Here, a reference direction is chosen as an arrow in the plane, often considered to point due east and interpreted as the \(x\)-axis of a coordinate system. Vectors are given as a length and an angle and considered arrows with that length and forming the final leg of the angle with the \(x\)-axis.

In that notation, a displacement might be given as \(10\text{m}\)\([25^\circ]\) indicating that an object was moved a distance of \(10\text{m}\) at an angle of \(25^\circ\) with respect to the reference direction.

While this is inuitive, it is difficult to describe the result of combined movements. That is where the cartesian representation comes in.

1.2. Cartesian Vectors

These are tuples \((x,y)\) in a cartesian coordinate system and interpreted as arrows pointing from the origin \((0,0)\) of the coordinate system to the point \((x,y)\).

In this representation, a displacement might be given as \((10\text{m},25\text{m})\) indicating that that an object was moved \(10\text{m}\) to the right on the \(x\)-axis and then \(25\text{m}\) up parallel to the \(y\)-axis. Here, there is an emphasis on consecutive movements, if the object is moved again by \((15\text{m},-2\text{m})\), that is another \(15\text{m}\) to the right parallel to the \(x\)-axis and \(2\text{m}\) down parallel to the \(y\)-axis, then it is easy to see that the result of the combined movement is \[(10\text{m},25\text{m})+(15\text{m},-2\text{m})=(10\text{m}+15\text{m},25\text{m}-2\text{m})=(25\text{m},23\text{m}).\] This is tantamount to a displacement of \(25\text{m}\) to the right and \(23\text{m}\) up.

1.2.1. Cartesian Vector Algebra

Addition
Cartesian vectors \(\pmb{v}=(v_1,v_2)\) and \(\pmb{u}=(u_1,u_2)\) can be added and subtracted component-wise according to \[(v_1,v_2)\pm(u_1,u_2)=(v_1\pm u_1,v_2\pm u_2).\]
Scalar Multiplication
A cartesian vector \(\pmb{v}=(v_1,v_2)\) can be multiplied by a scalar component-wise according to \[s(v_1,v_2)=(sv_1,sv_2).\] This has the effect of changing the length of the vector and, in the case of negative \(s\) changing its direction to the opposite.

1.2.2. The Length of a Cartesian Vector

Since a vector \(\pmb{v}=(v_1,v_2)\) is made up of two components \(v_1,\,v_2\) at right angles, its length is \[\|\pmb{v}\|=\sqrt{v_1^2+v_2^2}.\]

1.2.3. The Direction of a Cartesian Vector

A vector \(\pmb{v}=(v_1,v_2)\) is the final leg in an angle \(\alpha\) which has opposite \(v_2\) and adjacent \(v_1\), consequently \[\alpha=\tan^{-1}\frac{v_2}{v_1}.\] However, when using calculator results, only the angles for vectors pointing into Quadrant I are correct. For vectors pointing into Quadrants II and III, \(180^\circ\) has to be added to the calculator result and for vectors pointing into Quadrant IV, \(360^\circ\) has to be added.

1.2.4. Unit Vectors and Special Directions

Unit vectors are vectors with length \(1\). There are a few special unit vectors that are noteworthy.

Right
This is \((1,0)\), the unit vector pointing to the right along the \(x\)-axis.
Up
This is \((0,1)\), the unit vector pointing up along the \(y\)-axis.
Left
This is the opposite \((-1,0)\) to the vector pointing right, note that \((-1,0)=(-1)(1,0)\).
Down
The opposite \((0,-1)\) to the vector \((0,1)\) pointing up. Again, they are related by \((-1,0)=(-1)(0,1)\).

In general, unit vectors are of the form \[\pmb{u}_\alpha=(\cos\alpha,\sin\alpha),\] due to the trigonometry identity \(\cos^2\alpha+\sin^2\alpha=\). Such a vector \(\pmb{u}_\alpha\) is called the unit vector in direction \(\alpha\).

1.3. Conversion between Vector Representations

The conversion from vectors in polar representation to cartesian representation is easy when the polar reference direction is the positive \(x\)-axis: \[r[\alpha]\rightarrow r(\cos\alpha,\sin\alpha)=(r\cos\alpha,r\sin\alpha),\] which will mostly be the case. Thus a displacement \(10\text{m}[25^\circ]\) by distance \(10\text{m}\) in direction \(\alpha\) has cartesian representation \[(10\text{m}\cos 25^\circ,10\text{m}\sin 25^\circ)=(9.1\text{m},4.2\text{m}).\] For the conversion from cartesian representation \(\pmb{v}=(v_1,v_2)\) to polar representation \(r[\alpha]\) simply calculate \[r=\|\pmb{v}\|,\,\alpha=\tan^{-1}\frac{v_2}{v_1},\] with the necessary correction for \(\alpha\) as indicated above.
