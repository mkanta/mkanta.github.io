1. Kinematics

1.1. Falling Objects

Falling objects follow paths of the form \[\vec{d}(t)=\vec{v}_it+\frac{1}{2}\vec{a}t^2\] with acceleration \(\vec{a}=(0,-9.81)\) due to gravity, and initial velocity \[\vec{v}_i=(s\cos\alpha,s\sin\alpha).\] Here \(s\) is the speed of the object at launch and \(\alpha\) the cartesian angle of its direction at launch.

In many cases, this will be an angle with respect to the horizontal, which will be positive if the object is launched upward, negative if it is thrown down and \(0\) if it is launched horizontally.

Given the time \(t_0\) of its impact, the range of the object can then be read off from the first component of \(\vec{d}(t_0)\), its launch height from the second component of \(\vec{d}(t_0)\).

1.1.1. Initial Velocity

An object launched with speed \(s\) at an horizontal angle \(\alpha\) has initial velocity \[\vec{v}_i=(s\cos\alpha,s\sin\alpha).\]

  1. An object is launched at speed \(10.0\)m/s at an angle of \(-35.0^\circ\) with respect to the horizontal. Determine its initial velocity, its initial horizontal velocity and its initial vertical velocity.

    Click to see the answer, hints or generate more questions of this type.

    Initial velocity: \((8.19,-5.74)\) m/s, Horizontal:\(8.19\) m/s, Vertical: \(-5.74\) m/s

    1. Angles with respect to the horizontal are cartesian angles,
    2. the speed is the magnitude of the velocity,
    3. therefore the initial velocity is \[\vec{v}=(10\cos(-35),10\sin(-35))=(8.19, -5.74)\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}}.\]
    4. Its first component \(8.19\) m/s is the initial horizontal velocity.
    5. Its second component \(-5.74\) m/s is the initial vertical velocity.

1.1.2. Path of a Falling Object

  1. An object is launched at \(2.15\) m/s at \(-35.0^\circ\) to the horizontal. It hits the ground after \(0.500\) s. Determine its range and the height from which it was launched.

    Click to see the answer, hints or generate more questions of this type.

    Range: \(0.881\) m, Height at Launch:\(1.84\) m

    1. The initial velocity is given by \[\vec{v}_i=(2.15\cos(-35.0),2.15\sin(-35.0)),\]
    2. which results in a trajectory of \[(d_x,d_y)=(2.15\cos(-35.0),2.15\sin(-35.0))t+\frac{1}{2}(0,-9.81)t^2.\]
    3. At time \(t=0.500\) s, this becomes \[(d_x,d_y)=(2.15\cos(-35.0),2.15\sin(-35.0))0.500+\frac{1}{2}(0,-9.81)0.500^2,\]
    4. or \[(d_x,d_y)=(0.881, -1.84).\]
    5. This means its range is \(d_x=0.881\) m and
    6. \(d_y=-1.84\) indicates that it went \(1.84\) m down before impact, hence
    7. it was launched from a height of \(1.84\) m.

1.2. Projectiles

Since projectiles are essentially falling objects, their motion is again described by the equation \[\vec{d}(t)=\vec{v}_it+\frac{1}{2}\vec{a}t^2\] with 2-dimensional vectors \(\vec{d}=(d_x,d_y)\) describing displacement from an initial position, initial velocity \(\vec{v}_i=(v_x,v_y)\) and acceleration \(\vec{a}=(a_x,a_y)\). If the coordinate system is chosen to have its origin at the beginning of the motion then \(\vec{d}(t)\) can be interpreted as the position of the projectile at time \(t\) in this coordinate system.

Since projectile motion is falling motion, only the vertical component experiences acceleration caused by gravity, hence \[\vec{a}=(0,-9.81).\] The initial velocity \(\vec{v}\) given by an initial speed \(s\) at an angle \(\alpha\) with respect to the horizontal is \[\vec{v}=(s\cos\alpha,s\sin\alpha).\] In particular, an initial velocity in the horizontal direction is characterised by an angle \(\alpha=0\), reulting in a 2-dimensional vector \(\vec{v}_i=(s,0)\).

  1. An object is launched at \(15.0\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}}\) at an angle of \(30.0^\circ\) to the horizontal. Determine its range and maximum height.

    Click to see the answer, hints or generate more questions of this type.

    Range:\(19.9\)m, Height:\(2.87\)m

    1. The initial velocity is \(\vec{v}=(15.0\cos 30.0,15.0\sin 30.0)\) and the acceleration
    2. is \(\vec{a}=(0,-9.81)\). This motion is described by
    3. the equation \[(d_x,d_y)=(15.0\cos 30.0 ,15.0\sin 30.0 )t+\frac{1}{2}(0,-9.81)t^2,\]
    4. which has horizontal component \(d_x=15.0\cos 30.0 t\) and vertical component
    5. \(d_y=15.0\sin 30.0 t-\frac{1}{2}9.81t^2\).
    6. Since the range is to be determined, the time \(t\) has to be found when the object hits the ground, ie when the vertical displacement from the ground is \(d_y=0\).
    7. From the vertical component of the motion, this puts the requirement \[0=15.0\sin 30.0 t-\frac{1}{2}9.81t^2=t(15.0\sin 30.0 -\frac{1}{2}9.81t)\] on \(t\).
    8. This is satisfied when \(t=0\) or \[15.0\sin 30.0 -\frac{1}{2}9.81t=0,\]
    9. with the latter \(t\) being of interest because that represents the time when the object lands.
    10. Solving for \(t\), this becomes \[t=\frac{30.0\sin 30.0 }{9.81}.\]
    11. To find the range, this has to be inserted back into the horizontal component, which becomes \[d_x=15.0\cos 30.0 \frac{30.0\sin 30.0 }{9.81}=\frac{15.0\times 30.0\sin 30.0 \cos 30.0 }{9.81}=19.9,\] so the range is \(19.9\)m.
    12. For the height, \[0=\sin^230.0 15.0^2-2\times9.81\times d_y\] needs to be computed,
    13. which leads to \[d_y=\frac{15.0^2\sin^230.0}{2\times 9.81}=2.87,\] a height of \(2.87\)m.

1.3. Relative Motion

In relative motion problems, there are usually two velocities involved that have to be added up or subtracted.

A lot of these problems involve the velocity of an object with respect to a medium, such as that of a boat in water or an airplane in air, and the velocity of the medium with respect to to the ground. Examples are a boat in a river or an airplane in windy air.

In these cases, the two velocities have to be added up.

  1. A swimmer capable of swimming \(2.00\) m/s in still water crosses a river flowing \(30.0^\circ\) north of west at \(8.00\) m/s at \(90.0^\circ\). Determine the velocity of the swimmer relative to an observer at rest watching from a bank of the river.

    Click to see the answer, hints or generate more questions of this type.

    \(8.25\) m/s at \(44^\circ\) north of west.

    1. The direction of \(30.0^\circ\) north of west corresponds to a cartesian angle of \(180^\circ-30.0^\circ=150^\circ\), hence the velocity of the water with respect to ground is
    2. \[\vec{v}_1=(8.00\cos 150,8.0\sin 150)=(-6.93, 4.00)\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}}.\]
    3. The swimmer swims at an angle of \(90.0^\circ\) with respect to the river, hence at a cartesian angle of \(150^\circ-90.0^\circ=60.0^\circ\).
    4. Therefore, the swimmer's velocity vector is \[\vec{v}_2=(2.00\cos 60.0,2.00\sin 60.0)=(1.00, 1.73)\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}}.\]
    5. The velocity with repect to the stationary observer is \[\vec{v}_1+\vec{v}_2=(-6.93, 4.00)+(1.00, 1.73)=(-5.93, 5.73)\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}},\]which
    6. translates to \(8.25\) m/s at a cartesian angle of \(136^\circ\) or \(180^\circ-136^\circ=44^\circ\) north of west.

2. Dynamics

2.1. Pulling against Friction

In the simplest case, objects are pulled against some resisting force, such as friction or gravity. Three forces are involved here, the resisting force, the applied force and the net force. The latter determines the acceleration of the object according to Newton's second law.

The equation

\begin{equation}\label{dyn1 force equation} \vec{F}_\text{net}=\vec{F}_a+\vec{F}_f. \end{equation}

models this behaviour.

  1. How much force needs to be applied to a \(60\) kg object to accelerate it at \(4.0\text{m}/\text{s}^2\) if there is a friction force of \(40\) N?

    Click to see the answer, hints or generate more questions of this type.

    \(280\) N opposite to the friction force.

    1. With the applied force \(\vec{F}_a\) and the friction force \(\vec{F}_f\) acting on the object, the net force \(\vec{F}_\text{net}\) becomes, according to equation (\ref{dyn1 force equation}), \[ \vec{F}_\text{net}=\vec{F}_a+\vec{F}_f. \]
    2. Since the net force is the force accelerating the object, it is related to the acceleration by Newton's second law \[\vec{F}_\text{net}=m\vec{a}=4.0\times 60=240.\]
    3. The friction force always acts opposite to the net force, so with a positive friction force, the net force is negative
    4. and equation (\ref{dyn1 force equation}) becomes \[-240=\vec{F}_a+40.\]
    5. Solving for \(\vec{F}_a\) results in
    6. \(\vec{F}_a=-280\) N, hence
    7. a force of \(280\) N opposite to the friction force needs to be applied to the object.

2.2. Pulling at an Angle

Pulling an object at an angle across a floor has two effects: the effective applied force is reduced and the normal force is affected. This is due to the fact that the effective applied force is the horizontal component of the applied force. The vertical component, on the other hand, will either lift the object upwards and thus reduce the amount of force required to keep it on the surface, or pull it downwards with the opposite effect.

  1. A \(50\) kg object is dragged across a horizontal floor by applying a force of \(200\) N at an angle of \(30.0^\circ\) to the horizontal. If the coefficient of kinematic friction is \(\mu=0.300\), what is the acceleration experienced by the object?

    Click to see the answer, hints or generate more questions of this type.

    \(1.12\text{m}/\text{s}^2\)

    1. The net force is calculated as \[\vec{F}_\text{net}=\vec{F}_\text{ah}+\vec{F}_f,\] where \(\vec{F}_\text{ah}\) is the horizontal component of the applied force \[\vec{F}_a=(200\cos 30,200\sin 30).\]
    2. This force has horizontal component \[F_\text{ah}=200\cos 30=173\text{ N}.\]
    3. The friction is calculated, as usual, from the coefficient of kinematic friction \(\mu=0.300\) and the normal force \(\vec{F}_N\).
    4. Due to the vertical component \(\vec{F}_\text{av}\) of the applied force, the normal force, which is the force required to keep the object from falling through the surface, is reduced by this amount, hence \[\vec{F}_N=-(\vec{F}_g+\vec{F}_\text{av}),\]
    5. leaving a magnitude of \[|F_N|=-(-9.81\times 50+200\sin 30)=9.81\times 50-200\sin 30=391.\]
    6. Thus the magnitude of friction becomes \[|F_f|=0.300\times 391=117,\]
    7. and the force of friction amounts to \[\vec{F}_f=-117\text{ N}.\]
    8. This leads to a net force of \[\vec{F}_\text{net}=173-117=56\text{ N}.\] The acceleration then becomes
    9. \[\vec{a}=\frac{\vec{F}_\text{net}}{m}=\frac{56}{50}=1.12\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}^2}.\]

2.3. Pulling from Different Sides

In tug-of-war type situations, several forces act in different directions on an object. Again, the movement of the object is determined by the resulting net force according to Newton's second law.

With forces \(\vec{F}_1,\,\vec{F}_2,\ldots,\vec{F}_n\), the net force becomes the sum

\begin{equation}\label{dyn2 force equation} \vec{F}_\text{net}=\vec{F}_1+\vec{F}_2+\cdots+\vec{F}_n. \end{equation}

  1. A \(20\) kg object experiences a \(15\) N force pulling south, a \(25\) N force pulling west and a \(20\) N force pulling at \(30^\circ\) S of E. What is the resulting acceleration of the object?

    Click to see the answer, hints or generate more questions of this type.

    \(26\) N at \(73^\circ\) S of W.

    1. With the \(15\) N force pulling south represented by \[\vec{F}_1=15(0,-1)=(0,-15),\]
    2. the \(25\) N force pulling west represented by \[\vec{F}_2=25(-1,0)=(-25,0),\]
    3. and the \(20\) N force at \(30^\circ\) S of E represented by \[\vec{F}_3=20(\cos 330,\sin 330)=(17.3, -10.0)\]
    4. the net force is calculated according to equation (\ref{dyn2 force equation}) \[\vec{F}_\text{net}=\vec{F}_1+\vec{F}_2+\vec{F}_3\] and becomes
    5. \[\vec{F}_\text{net}=(-25,-15)+(17,-10)=\(-7.7, -25\),\] which correponds to
    6. a force of \(26\) N at \(73\^ˆ) S of W.

2.4. Kinematics of Pulled Objects

Eventually, the goal in all the dynamics exercises is the calculation of the movement of objects subject to certain forces. This is achieved by calculating the net force and applying Newton's second law to determine the acceleration caused by it.

  1. A force of \(130\) N East is applied for \(2.0\) seconds to a \(50\) kg object initially at rest. What is its displacement?

    Click to see the answer, hints or generate more questions of this type.

    \(5.2\) m East.

    1. Since no other forces are involved, the applied force is the same as the net force and provides the acceleration of the object as \[\vec{a}=\frac{{F}_\text{net}}{m}=\frac{130}{50}=2.6\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}^2}\text{East}.\]
    2. The displacement then follows from \[\vec{d}=\vec{v}_it+\frac{1}{2}\vec{a}t^2,\]
    3. which evaluates to \[\vec{d}=0+\vec{1}{2}\times 2.6\times 2.0^2=5.2\text{ m East}.\]

  1. A force of \(60\) N East is applied for \(5.0\) s to a \(15\) kg object travelling East at \(50\) m/s. What is the resulting velocity if there is a friction force of \(20\) N?

    Click to see the answer, hints or generate more questions of this type.

    \(63\) m/s East.

    1. The forces involved here are an applied force of \(60\) N East and a counteracting friction force of \(20\) N West, so the net force is
    2. \[\vec{F}_\text{net}=60-20=40\text{ N East}.\]
    3. The net force provides, as always, the acceleration according to Newton's second law as
    4. \[\vec{a}=\frac{\vec{F}_\text{net}}{m}=\frac{40}{15}=2.7\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}^2}\text{ East}.\]
    5. With \[\vec{v}(t)=\vec{v}_i+\vec{a}t,\]
    6. this results in a velocity of
    7. \[\vec{v}=50+2.7\times 5.0=63\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}}\text{ East}.\]

2.5. Tension and Compound Objects

These exercises are about objects connected by strings or ropes and, in addition to calculating the kinematic characteristics of the combination of these objects, the task here involves the determination of the tension in the strings connecting the objects.

Remember that the tension in the string between objects is the applied force on the object that is pulled, so the net force acting on it is related to the tension and friction by \[\vec{F}_\text{net}=\vec{F}_T+\vec{F}_f,\] so \[\vec{F}_T=\vec{F}_\text{net}-\vec{F}_f.\] The net force \(\vec{F}_\text{net}\) acting on the object can be calculated from its mass and the acceleration of the total system.

  1. A \(100\) kg hanging object is attached to a \(200\) kg object on a table by a rope guided by a frictionless wheel at the end of the table. If the coefficient of friction of the table is \(\mu=0.25\), what is the tension in the rope.
    1. The magnitude of force applied to this system is the force acting on the hanging object due to gravity, ie \(|\vec{F}_a|=9.81\times 100=981\) N.
    2. The force of friction counteracting the applied force is \[|\vec{F}_f|=\mu\vec{F}_n=0.25\times 9.81\times 200=491\text{ N}.\]
    3. The net force acting on the system is \[\vec{F}_\text{net}=\vec{F}_a-\vec{F}_f=981-491=490\text{ N}.\]
    4. The tension in the string is the force applied to the \(200\) kg object on the table, which can be obtained from \[\vec{F}_\text{net}=\vec{F}_a+\vec{F}_f,\] once the net force on the \(200\) kg object is known
    5. This net force can be determined from the acceleration of the total system \[\vec{a}=\frac{490}{200+100}=1.63\,\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}^2}\] and the mass of \(200\) kg as
    6. \(\vec{F}_\text{net}=200\times 1.63=326\) N
    7. The friction force on the object is still \(491\) N, hence
    8. the equation for the applied force is \[326=\vec{F}_a-491\text{ N}\] and the tension in the string is
    9. \(\vec{F}_a=\vec{F}_T=326+491=817\) N
